Summary

Missing person is an extremely complex issue that causes pain to hundreds of thousands of people every year around the world. According to estimates from the World Health Organization (WHO), over 600,000 people go missing every year.

We wanted to analyze the data to look into demographic relationships of missing persons and assess if certain characteristics such as time of year, gender, race, age, location, are more likely to result in a missing person becoming a long-term missing person.

What We Learned


  • Over 99% of missing persons are found or return home within 48 hours.
  • A person remains on the missing persons file until they are found dead or alive. For example, if 1000 people are reported missing, and 700 were found or returned home, only 300 would show up on the misisng persons list for that year.
  • Males are more likely than females to be missing persons.Native Americans and Blacks have higher than normal rates of disappearance.
  • Individuals in the late teens to early thirties are disproportionately likely to become missing.
  • Missing people tend to disappear in warmer months, with a modest increase in the winter during the holidays.
  • More sparsely populated states generally have higher rates of disappearance, but missing persons are tending to normalized toward the national average.
